The Dopamine Menu — the wellness trend everyone is talking about
💡 Tip Words in purple are today's vocabulary. Hover or tap them to see the meaning!

Imagine you are having one of those days. You feel flat, unmotivatedLacking the energy or desire to do things., and you can not figure out why. You are not sick. Nothing terrible happened. You are just… stuck. So you pick up your phone and start scrolling. An hour later, you feel worse. Sound familiar?

This is the problem that the dopamine menuA personalised list of activities designed to boost your mood in a healthy way. is trying to solve. The concept is simple: instead of reaching for your phone the moment you feel bored or low, you consult a personal list of activities — organised like a restaurant menu — that are designed to make you feel genuinely better. Not for two minutes. Actually better.

✦

The idea was first created in 2020 by Jessica McCabe, a YouTube creator with ADHD, as a tool to help people with attention difficulties manage their energy and mood. But it went viralSpreading very quickly on social media, seen by millions of people. on TikTok in the years that followed, and by 2025 it had become one of the most discussed wellness concepts online. The reason? It turns out the problem it was designed to solve — struggling to feel good without a screen — is not just an ADHD problem. It is a very human one.

The neurotransmitterA chemical in the brain that sends signals between nerve cells. Dopamine is one of them. dopamine is often called the brain's "feel-good chemical." But that is a misconceptionA wrong belief or idea about something.. Dopamine is not actually released when you feel pleasure — it is released in anticipationThe feeling of excitement or expectation about something that is going to happen. of it. It is the chemical of wanting, not having. This is why scrolling social media is so addictive: every refresh is a tiny hit of dopamine, the brain asking "what will I find next?" — and never quite getting satisfied.

"Social media apps can cause the release of large amounts of dopamine — just like heroin, meth, or alcohol. They amplify the feel-good properties that attract humans to each other."

— Dr. Anna Lembke, author of Dopamine Nation, Stanford University
✦

The dopamine menu works by giving you a curatedCarefully chosen and organised for a specific purpose. set of alternatives — activities that produce real, sustainable dopamine rather than quick spikes followed by crashes. Like a restaurant menu, it is divided into sections based on how much time and energy each activity requires.

A dopamine menu — example
Personalise yours with what actually brings you joy
🥗 Starters — quick, under 5 mins
  • Make a cup of coffee or tea
  • Look at a photo that makes you smile
  • Step outside for fresh air
  • Stretch for 2 minutes
🍽️ Mains — need 20–60 mins
  • Go for a walk without your phone
  • Cook something you enjoy
  • Call a friend or family member
  • Read a chapter of a book
🥡 Sides — pair with other things
  • Listen to a playlist you love
  • Light a candle while you work
  • Open a window, let in natural light
  • Doodle or colour while watching TV
🍰 Specials — once in a while
  • A day trip somewhere new
  • A concert or live event
  • A long dinner with people you love
  • Start a new creative project

The key distinctionAn important difference between two things. between a dopamine menu and a simple to-do list is this: the activities are not things you have to do. They are things you genuinelyTruly and sincerely — not just pretending. enjoy. The whole point is to design something that feels like a treat, not a chore. You create it on a good day, when you have the energy to think clearly — so that on a hard day, all you have to do is choose from the menu.

Critics point out that the concept is not exactly revolutionary — humans have always known that going for a walk or calling a friend feels better than staring at a screen. But supporters argue that the menu format does something useful: it removes the cognitive loadThe mental effort needed to make decisions and think. of deciding what to do when your brain is already depleted. You do not have to think. You just pick a dish.

"The best time to make your dopamine menu is when you don't feel like you need it — you'll be creative, you'll have energy, and you'll actually enjoy making it."

— Quoted in The Good Trade, 2026

Whether or not you believe in the science behind it, the dopamine menu raises a question worth sitting with: when was the last time you felt genuinely good — not just distracted? And what, specifically, caused that feeling? The answer, it turns out, might be more interesting than anything on your feed.
