The Office Is Calling —
But Does Anyone Want to Answer?

31 días de inglés real · March 2026 · 5 min read
🏢

It is 8:47 on a Monday morning. Somewhere in Buenos Aires, a professional is sitting at their kitchen table in pyjamas, laptop open, coffee in hand — already in their second meeting of the day. Somewhere in New York, their colleague is stuck in rush hourThe busy time of day when many people travel to or from work, causing traffic. traffic, forty minutes from the office, running late. Who has it better? That question — simple on the surface — sits at the heart of one of the biggest workplace debatesDiscussions where people express different or opposing opinions about a topic. of our time.

Between 2020 and 2023, remote work went from being a rare privilege to the new normal almost overnight. The COVID-19 pandemic forced companies to adapt, and millions of people discovered something surprising: work, for many jobs, did not require an office at all. Productivity stayed up. Commutes disappeared. People reclaimed hours of their day.

But since 2023, the pendulumA weight that swings back and forth — used figuratively to mean something that shifts between two extremes. has started to swing back. Major companies — Amazon, JPMorgan, Instagram, TikTok, Microsoft — have issued mandatesOfficial orders or instructions that must be followed. requiring employees to return to the office, some for five full days a week. The message from the top was clear: the remote work experiment is over. Employees disagreed.

The data is striking. In 2026, only 9% of knowledge workersPeople whose jobs involve thinking, creating and managing information — not physical labour. — those in professional, office-based roles — are back in the office five days a week. The most common arrangement is now hybridA mix of working from home and working in the office.: three days in, two days remote. And despite the headlines about return-to-office mandatesCompany policies that require employees to come back to the office after working remotely., remote work is actually more common in 2026 than it was in 2023.

So why are companies pushing so hard to bring people back? The arguments on both sides are real — and they say something interesting about what we value in work, and in life.

The case for the office
  • Spontaneous conversations spark creative ideas
  • Junior employees learn faster near senior colleagues
  • Relationships are easier to build face to face
  • Harder to feel isolated or disconnected
  • Clearer separation between work and home life
The case for remote
  • Workers save an average of 55 minutes per day — no commute
  • Remote workers are 10–15% more productive on focused tasks
  • Companies access talent from anywhere in the world
  • Better work-life balance, especially for parents and carers
  • Lower carbon footprint — fewer people travelling daily

One of the most compellingSo convincing or interesting that it is hard to ignore. findings of recent research is this: the debate may be generational. Gen Z workers — those born after 1996 — are more likely to want to go to the office than Millennials or Gen X. They crave mentorshipGuidance and support from a more experienced person to help someone develop their skills., structure and in-person connection. They grew up with screens and, perhaps because of that, they value real-world interaction more than their older colleagues do.

"After nearly six years of hybrid work, younger employees are coming to the office craving mentorship, connection and growth."

— Built In, January 2026
✦

What seems clear is that the binary question — office or home? — is the wrong one. The real question is more nuanced: what kind of work benefits from being together, and what kind is better done alone? Deep, focused work — writing, coding, analysing — tends to go better at home. Creative collaborationWorking together with others to achieve something., onboarding, and relationship-building tend to go better in person.

The future of work is not a location. It is a question about what we actually need to do our best work — and whether anyone is brave enough to ask it honestly.
