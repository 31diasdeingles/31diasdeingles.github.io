Step 1
Arriving and getting a table
What you might say
More formal
More natural ✓
I would like a table for two people, please.
A table for two, please.
I have a reservation under the name of Smith.
I have a reservation. It's under Smith.
Is it possible to sit near the window?
Could we sit near the window?
💡 Note

You don't need to say "I would like a table" every time — in spoken English, shorter is more natural. The waiter will understand "Table for two, please" perfectly.

✦
Step 2
Ordering food and drinks
The most useful structures
Structure
Example
I'll have...
"I'll have the pasta, please."
I'd like...
"I'd like a glass of sparkling water."
Can I get...
"Can I get the steak, medium rare?"
Could I have...
"Could I have the soup as a starter?"

✓ All four are correct and natural. "I'll have" and "Can I get" are the most common in everyday conversation. Avoid "I want" — it can sound rude.

Asking questions about the menu
Situation
What to say
You don't know what something is
"What is the risotto exactly?" / "What does it come with?"
You want a recommendation
"What would you recommend?" / "What's popular here?"
You have dietary restrictions
"Does this have gluten?" / "Is this dish vegetarian?"
You need more time
"Could we have a few more minutes?" / "We're not quite ready yet."

Step 3
During the meal and paying
Useful phrases at the table
Situation
What to say
Something is missing
"Excuse me, I think I ordered the salmon, not the chicken."
You want something extra
"Could I get some more bread, please?" / "Can we get some extra napkins?"
Asking for the bill
"Could we get the bill, please?" / "Can I have the check?"
Splitting the bill
"Can we pay separately?" / "Could we split the bill?"
🇺🇸 US vs 🇬🇧 UK

In the US, people say "check" — "Can I get the check?" In the UK and most of the world, people say "bill" — "Could we have the bill?" Both are correct. If you travel, you'll hear both.

Now read
A full conversation — at the restaurant

Read the dialogue below. The phrases in green are the key structures from today's lesson.

📍 Scene: A restaurant. Sofia and Marco arrive for dinner.
Waiter
Good evening! Do you have a reservation?
Sofia
Yes, we have a reservation — it's under Rivera, for two.
Waiter
Perfect. Right this way. Here are your menus. Can I get you anything to drink while you look?
Marco
I'll have a sparkling water, please.
Sofia
And could I get a glass of the house red?
Waiter
Of course. I'll be back in a moment. *(returns)* Are you ready to order?
Sofia
Almost — what would you recommend for the main course?
Waiter
The sea bass is very popular tonight. It comes with roasted vegetables and a lemon butter sauce.
Sofia
That sounds great. I'll have the sea bass, then. Does it come with any sides?
Waiter
Yes, the vegetables are included. Would you like a starter?
Sofia
No, thank you. I'd like the green salad on the side instead, if that's okay.
Waiter
Of course. And for you, sir?
Marco
I'll have the mushroom risotto. Is it vegetarian?
Waiter
Yes, completely. No meat or fish.
Marco
Perfect. That's all for now, thank you.
Waiter
*(later)* Can I get you anything else? Dessert?
Marco
Not for me, thanks. Could we get the bill, please?
Sofia
And could we pay separately?
Waiter
Of course! I'll bring two bills right away.

Today's vocabulary
8 key words and phrases from this lesson
Reservation
A booking made in advance to guarantee a table at a restaurant.
Starter / Main / Dessert
The three courses of a meal: first dish, main dish, and sweet final dish.
To come with
To be served together with something. "Does it come with fries?"
Side (dish)
A small extra dish served alongside the main course.
Dietary restriction
A food limitation for health, allergy, or personal reasons.
The bill / the check
The document showing how much you owe. "Bill" = UK, "check" = US.
To split the bill
To divide the total cost between the people at the table.
To recommend
To suggest something as being good. "What would you recommend?"
